Fertility Rate Analysis

The objective of this project is to conduct exploratory data analysis on the key contributing factors and symptoms of a country with low fertility rate. We will examine the relationship between each country’s indicators of development such as literacy rates, infant mortality, economic achievements, and fertility rate.

An interactive R Shiny app will be designed to see if the trade-off for achieving economic and societal successes, is an incapacitated fertility rate. The application will help policy makers better understand how a country’s level of development can affect their fertility rate, and potentially provide guidance on the necessary actions to prevent a further decline in fertility rate.

2. Data Preparation

(1) 25 Countries with missing fertility rates were excluded (Serbia, Andorra, Isle of man etc)

Variables

(1) Countries are classified as Advanced Countries, and Emerging and Developing Countries in accordance to International Monetary Fund’s definition (Advocates for International Development, n.d.).

3. Assumptions:

(1) Period of data collection (for each year) by different sources are assumed to be identical
(2) Data collected from all countries are accurate
(3) Census data includes all citizens

Advanced countries like Singapore are described with constrictive population pyramids (inverted shape with graph tapering in at the bottom) – suggesting that there is a lower mortality rate, decreasing fertility rate (Geographic, n.d.). Emerging and developing countries like Ghana are described with expansive population pyramids (broad base and narrow top) – suggesting that the population is young and growing. The population age structure pyramid can be broken down into 3 segments:
• Pre-Reproductive: Birth to Age 14 years. When most females are pre-pubescent.
• Reproductive: Age 15 – 44 years. This age range is known to be a woman’s child-bearing years.
• Post-Reproductive: Age 45 to end of life. These are the years when a woman’s fertility declines.

The World Health Organisation says that the natural sex ratio is 105 males to 100 females (Human Rights Watch, n.d.). The same may not be observed for a more liberal society.

5.2. Trend of Fertility Rate

To project fertility rate to year 2050. Linear and exponential regression on a time-series analysis was used, grouped by advanced & emerging economies as a group.
The lm function in R was used to obtain both linear and exponential regression’s intercept and gradient respectively.

Upon further examining Figure 9, we conclude that the linear projection for emerging economies’ countries is not a good model as the rate of change of fertility rate for emerging countries is large. As emerging countries tend to develop quickly, fertility rates were decreasing at a much faster than the advanced countries. Using a simple linear model, the obtained projected mean fertility rate of the emerging & developing countries is 0.44 in the year of 2050, which is lower than the projected fertility rate of advanced countries in the same year, at 1.39. If further years are to be projected, linear regression model would be projecting a negative fertility instead.

As emerging countries become advanced over time, we project the rate of change of fertility rate would decrease and tend towards that of advanced countries, plateauing towards a single value. Therefore, the team has used the exponential regression instead to better represent the projection for fertility rate. 5.3. Correlation between Fertility Rate and Identified Factors

Pearson correlation is conducted between Fertility and the 3 identified factors across all countries. The 3 factors are GDP, education rate and life expectancy. Using Pearson’s correlation, we find a moderate negative correlation between GDP per capita and fertility (r = -0.452), whereas there is a high negative correlation between education rate and fertility (r = -0.842), life expectancy and fertility (r = -0.844).
In the R Shiny app, we assess similar correlation factor for individual countries. For example, we find the highest negative correlation between GDP per capita and fertility in Singapore (r = -0.973) and Japan (r = -0.944) amongst advanced economies. Cyprus has the highest negative correlation between education rate and fertility (r = -0.889) and life expectancy and fertility (r = -0.948), amongst advanced economies.

5.4. Hypothesis Testing

In this segment, hypothesis tests will be conducted at level of significance , α of 5%, to discover whether it can be concluded that advanced countries, and emerging and developing countries have a fertility rate above 2.

Hypotheses:
• ‘Advanced’ countries have low fertility rate.

H_0: μ≤2
H_1:μ>2 where μ is the population mean (fertility rate) of advanced countries.
From test results, since the p-value = 1 is more than our alpha value of 0.05, we do not reject the null hypothesis. We have sufficient evidence that the mean of advanced countries’ fertility rate is less than or equals to 2. Therefore, we can conclude that advanced countries have lower fertility rate.

• ‘Emerging and developing’ countries have high fertility rate.

H_0: μ≤2
H_1:μ>2 where μ is the population mean (fertility rate) of ‘emerging and developing’ countries.
From test results, since p-value < 2.2e-16 is smaller than our alpha value of 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. There is sufficient evidence to conclude that emerging and developing countries have a fertility rate above 2. Therefore, we can conclude that emerging and developing countries have higher fertility rate.

From these hypothesis tests, we can conclude that emerging and developing countries have relatively higher fertility rate compared to advanced countries.

5.5. Linear Regression

The linear regression test is to see whether each factor is significant, in relation with our dependent variable, fertility rate, in advanced and emerging countries.

Hypotheses:
• Fertility rate of a country is highly correlated to GDP per capita.

H_0: Linear regression is not significant
H_1: Linear regression is significant
From test results, the linear regression for fertility rate with GDP per capita is significant in both types of countries, since the p-value from F statistics is less than 0.05. However, the adjusted R-squared in advanced countries is quite low, around 0.26 in the model. Given this, although the individual coefficients are significant, we cannot conclude that this linear regression model in advanced countries has a level of significance. We can only conclude that this linear regression model for emerging and developing countries is significant.

• Fertility rate of a country is highly correlated to life expectancy.

H_0: Linear regression is not significant
H_1: Linear regression is significant
From test results, the linear regression for fertility rate with life expectancy is significant in both types of countries since the p-value from F statistics is less than 0.05. Nonetheless, only 34% of the variations on the dependent variable can be explained by the factor of life expectancy, which is quite low. Thus, we cannot conclude that this linear regression model in advanced countries is significant despite the individual coefficients are significant. We can only conclude that this linear regression model for emerging and developing countries is significant.

• Fertility rate of a country is highly correlated to education rate.

H_0: Linear regression is not significant
H_1: Linear regression is significant
From test result seen in Figure 18, the linear regression for fertility rate with education rate is significant in both types of countries. In addition, the individual coefficients are also significant, making a high the adjusted R-squared.

5.6. Multi-Linear Regression

To find out which factor (GDP, Life Expectancy, Education) is least significant:

The removal of independent variable life expectancy has allowed the new multilinear regression to have a better statistical representation.
• Both intercept and both slopes (GDP & Edu) are statistically significant

From the test results, the adjusted R-squared is at 96.78%. Representing the proportion of the total variation in the dependent variable explained by the variation in the independent variables.

5.7. Chi-Square Testing

Additionally, chi-square tests were conducted to further understand the effect of GDP per capita, life expectancy and level of education on fertility rates across the world.
We divide the fertility rate and other three factors into 2x2 table and count how many countries belong to the responding group to execute the Chi-Square test. The category of the fertility rate is same as what we did in hypothesis test for advanced countries and emerging and developing countries, which is μ≤2 and μ>2.

Chi square test categorisation:
• GDP per capita is based on the average of the world in 2020 (≤$11k and >$11k).
• Education rate is based on the average secondary school enrolment rate around the world (≤85% and >85%).
• Life expectancy is based on the average life expectancy around the world in 2021 (≤70 years old and >70 years old)

Hypotheses:
• Does GDP per capita affect fertility rate?

H_0: No association exists between GDP and fertility rate
H_1: Association exists between GDP and fertility rate
From test results, the p-value = 1.361e-08 < α = 0.05, meaning that the GDP per capita is dependent on the fertility rate.

• Does level of education of the population affect the fertility rate?

H_0: No association exists between the level of education and fertility rate
H_1: Association exists between the level of education and fertility rate
From test results, the p-value = 0.3388 > α = 0.05, representing that the education rate is not dependent on the fertility rate.

• Does Life Expectancy affect the fertility rate?

H_0: No association exists between life expectancy and fertility rate
H_1: Association exists between life expectancy and fertility rate
From test results, the p-value = 0.01605 < α = 0.05, representing that the life expectancy is dependent on the fertility rate.

6. Conclusion

Based on the analysis above, it is not conclusive that the factors identified in this study are all negatively correlated to fertility rate, as we found that it differs across countries. It is also concluded that most advanced countries are experiencing low fertility rates, which would likely lead to an ageing population.
From the simple linear regression, it is discovered that for advanced countries: linear regression for GDP per capita and life expectancy was found to be not significant because of the very low R-squared, except for education rate. For emerging and developing countries, all factors were found to be significant, with GDP per capita being the least significant. However, when all three factors are looked at together, using a multi-linear regression, the life expectancy of the population is found to have contributed the least to the model. However, life expectancy is also found to have association with fertility rate, as life expectancy could be dependent on other factors that are relevant.
It was also found that education rate and fertility rate have strong linear relationship, but a ‘no association’ outcome was derived from the chi-square test. This could be due to missing data in the dataset, resulting in an inaccurate test. With sufficient data, the result may differ.
This study also serves to allow policy makers to identify, with ease, the country they would like to emulate when making policies and seeking economic growth for their own.
